Case
Mrs. Thompson is a 62-year-old female who works as a librarian and has a history of rheumatoid arthritis, for which she has been receiving immunosuppressive therapy. She presents to the clinic with a 5-day history of worsening pain and swelling in her left knee, accompanied by fever and chills. She reports that the knee pain began as a mild discomfort but has progressively intensified, making it difficult for her to walk or stand for long periods. She also notes that the skin over her knee has become red and warm to the touch. Mrs. Thompson mentions that she had a minor scrape on her knee about a week ago while gardening, which she initially dismissed as inconsequential. Given her immunosuppressive treatment and the recent skin injury, there is a concern for a possible staphylococcal infection, potentially involving Staphylococcus aureus, which is known for causing both superficial and deep-seated infections, including septic arthritis.

Mrs. Thompson has a significant medical history of rheumatoid arthritis, for which she has been on long-term immunosuppressive therapy, including methotrexate and low-dose prednisone. She denies any known drug allergies. Her social history reveals that she lives alone and is an avid gardener, which she finds therapeutic. She does not smoke or consume alcohol. There is no recent travel history, and she has not been in contact with anyone known to be ill. Her immunosuppressive state and recent skin injury while gardening are notable risk factors for a staphylococcal infection, particularly given her exposure to soil and potential skin flora.

On physical examination, Mrs. Thompson appears mildly distressed due to pain. Her vital signs show a temperature of 101.8°F, blood pressure of 130/78 mmHg, heart rate of 98 bpm, respiratory rate of 18 breaths per minute, and oxygen saturation of 97% on room air. The left knee is visibly swollen, with erythema extending approximately 5 cm beyond the patella. The area is warm to the touch and exquisitely tender, with a limited range of motion due to pain. There is no evidence of fluctuance or purulent discharge. The rest of the musculoskeletal examination is unremarkable, with no other joints showing signs of active inflammation. Cardiovascular, respiratory, and abdominal examinations are within normal limits. Given these findings, the clinical picture is highly suggestive of septic arthritis, likely due to Staphylococcus aureus, necessitating further diagnostic workup and prompt intervention.

Initial laboratory tests for Mrs. Thompson reveal a white blood cell count of 16,500/μL with 88% neutrophils, indicating a significant leukocytosis with a left shift. Hemoglobin is 11.2 g/dL, and platelets are 320,000/μL. Inflammatory markers are markedly elevated, with a C-reactive protein level of 18 mg/dL and an erythrocyte sedimentation rate of 65 mm/hr. The basic metabolic panel is within normal limits, except for a mild elevation in creatinine at 1.3 mg/dL, likely due to dehydration from fever.

An X-ray of the left knee shows soft tissue swelling but no bony abnormalities or joint effusion. To further evaluate the joint, an ultrasound is performed, revealing a moderate effusion in the left knee joint, consistent with synovitis. This finding prompts an arthrocentesis, which yields cloudy synovial fluid. The synovial fluid analysis shows a white blood cell count of 75,000/μL with 90% neutrophils, confirming a septic process.

Microbiological studies are crucial for definitive diagnosis. Gram stain of the synovial fluid shows numerous neutrophils and gram-positive cocci in clusters, suggestive of Staphylococcus species. Synovial fluid cultures grow Staphylococcus aureus within 24 hours. Further testing with a coagulase test confirms the presence of Staphylococcus aureus. Antimicrobial susceptibility testing reveals that the isolate is methicillin-sensitive (MSSA), showing susceptibility to oxacillin, cefazolin, and vancomycin, but resistance to penicillin. Blood cultures taken at the time of admission are negative, likely due to the localized nature of the infection and prior antibiotic exposure.

These findings confirm the diagnosis of septic arthritis due to methicillin-sensitive Staphylococcus aureus, necessitating targeted antibiotic therapy and possible surgical intervention to prevent joint damage.

The diagnosis of septic arthritis in Mrs. Thompson is confirmed, caused by methicillin-sensitive Staphylococcus aureus (MSSA). This diagnosis is supported by the clinical presentation of knee pain, swelling, and fever, along with laboratory findings of elevated inflammatory markers and synovial fluid analysis showing gram-positive cocci in clusters. The synovial fluid culture growing MSSA further corroborates the diagnosis.

The treatment plan involves initiating intravenous antibiotic therapy with cefazolin at a dose of 2 grams every 8 hours, given the organism's susceptibility profile. This regimen is chosen for its efficacy against MSSA and will be continued for a total of 4 to 6 weeks, depending on clinical response and follow-up evaluations. In addition to antibiotics, Mrs. Thompson will undergo joint aspiration to relieve pressure and remove purulent material, which may need to be repeated if significant effusion persists. 

Supportive care includes pain management with acetaminophen and careful monitoring of her renal function due to the mild elevation in creatinine. Her immunosuppressive therapy will be reviewed, and methotrexate may be temporarily withheld to allow for better infection control, with close coordination with her rheumatologist. 

Regular monitoring of inflammatory markers, renal function, and clinical symptoms will guide the duration of intravenous therapy. Transition to oral antibiotics, such as cephalexin, may be considered once she shows significant clinical improvement and is afebrile for at least 48 hours. Follow-up appointments will be scheduled to assess joint function and ensure resolution of the infection, with physical therapy introduced as needed to restore mobility and strength in the affected knee.

Key Concepts
- **Microbiological Characteristics**: Staphylococcus aureus is a gram-positive cocci that forms clusters and is coagulase-positive, distinguishing it from other staphylococcal species. It is a common colonizer of the skin and mucous membranes, particularly the anterior nares.

- **Pathophysiology**: S. aureus can cause both superficial and deep-seated infections, such as septic arthritis, by invading tissues and evading the immune system through various virulence factors, including toxins and adhesins.

- **Diagnostic Approaches**: Diagnosis of S. aureus infections involves clinical assessment, laboratory tests showing leukocytosis and elevated inflammatory markers, and microbiological confirmation through culture and Gram stain of affected tissues or fluids.

- **Treatment Principles**: Management of MSSA infections like septic arthritis includes targeted antibiotic therapy, such as intravenous cefazolin, and may require surgical intervention like joint aspiration to remove infected material.

- **Antibiotic Susceptibility**: Understanding the susceptibility profile of S. aureus, particularly distinguishing between methicillin-sensitive and methicillin-resistant strains, is crucial for selecting appropriate antibiotic therapy.

- **Prevention Strategies**: Preventive measures include proper wound care, especially in immunocompromised individuals, and monitoring for early signs of infection to prevent progression to more severe disease.

- **Complications and Management**: Potential complications of septic arthritis include joint damage and systemic spread, necessitating prompt treatment and regular monitoring of inflammatory markers and renal function to guide therapy duration and prevent recurrence.
