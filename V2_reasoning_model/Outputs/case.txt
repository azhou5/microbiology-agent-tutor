# CLINICAL CASE: STAPHYLOCOCCUS AUREUS INFECTION

## PATIENT INFORMATION
**Patient Profile:**

- **Name:** James Whitfield
- **Age:** 56
- **Gender:** Male
- **Relevant Demographics:** Lives in a suburban area outside of Chicago, Illinois. Works as a construction foreman. Non-smoker and occasional alcohol consumer. No recent travel history outside the United States.

- **Medical History:**
  - **Past Surgical History:** Underwent coronary artery bypass grafting (CABG) surgery six months ago. No complications reported post-operatively.
  - **Chronic Conditions:** Managed hypertension for the past 10 years. Type 2 diabetes mellitus diagnosed three years ago, currently on oral hypoglycemic agents (Metformin).
  - **Allergies:** No known drug allergies.

- **Recent Symptoms:**
  - Fever (up to 101°F) persistent over the past week.
  - Increasing fatigue and malaise.
  - Localized redness, warmth, and swelling on the left leg.

- **Risk Factors:**
  - Diabetes mellitus, which may predispose him to infections due to potential compromised immune response.
  - Recent hospitalization and cardiac surgery, which increase the risk of nosocomial infections.
  - Occupational hazard due to frequent cuts and scrapes from the construction environment.
  - Regular use of gym facilities, including shared equipment and locker rooms.

This constellation of factors—a recent surgical procedure, underlying chronic conditions, and potential exposure at work and the gym—collectively contribute to James's vulnerability to infections such as those caused by *Staphylococcus aureus*.

## HISTORY AND PHYSICAL EXAMINATION
**History of Present Illness:**

James Whitfield, a 56-year-old male, presents with a one-week history of fever and increasing fatigue. He reports feeling generally unwell, with persistent malaise over the past seven days. His fever initially measured 100°F but has spiked to 101°F periodically, particularly in the evenings. James notes a significantly reduced energy level, impacting his ability to perform his usual duties as a construction foreman.

Four days ago, he began to experience progressive redness, warmth, and noticeable swelling on his left lower leg, centered around a small abrasion inflicted in the workplace approximately two weeks ago. The lesion, initially minor and superficially healed, has become markedly tender and increasingly painful over the last 48 hours.

James denies having chills, nausea, vomiting, or any respiratory symptoms but mentions a poor appetite and difficulty sleeping due to discomfort from his leg. He has attempted symptomatic relief with over-the-counter acetaminophen, noting minimal improvement in fever and pain.

**Vital Signs:**

- Temperature: 101.2°F (38.4°C)
- Pulse: 92 beats per minute
- Respiratory Rate: 18 breaths per minute
- Blood Pressure: 136/85 mmHg
- Oxygen Saturation: 98% on room air

**Physical Examination:**

- **General Appearance:** James appears slightly diaphoretic and is mildly distressed due to discomfort. He is alert and oriented to person, place, and time.
  
- **Skin:** Examination reveals a 5 cm area of erythema on the anterior aspect of the left lower leg, surrounding a healing abrasion. The area is warm to touch, with a poorly demarcated border and slight edema. There is a central fluctuant region indicative of abscess formation. No lymphangitic streaking is present, but there is tenderness upon palpation.

- **Cardiovascular:** Regular rate and rhythm, no murmurs, rubs, or gallops noted.

- **Respiratory:** Clear to auscultation bilaterally; no wheezes, crackles, or respiratory distress observed.

- **Abdomen:** Soft, non-tender, non-distended with normal bowel sounds. 

- **Extremities:** No peripheral cyanosis or clubbing. Pulses are present and symmetrical in all extremities. No other skin lesions or signs of infection are noted elsewhere.

- **Neurological Examination:** Cranial nerves II-XII are intact. Muscle strength is 5/5 in all extremities, and sensation is intact. No focal neurological deficits observed.

The physical findings, combined with his recent history of an abrasion and systemic symptoms, suggest a localized staphylococcal infection, possibly complicated by cellulitis with abscess formation.

## DIAGNOSTIC STUDIES
**Diagnostic Studies:**

To accurately diagnose and assess the extent of James Whitfield's suspected *Staphylococcus aureus* infection, the following diagnostic studies are undertaken:

1. **Laboratory Results:**
   - **Complete Blood Count (CBC):** 
     - White Blood Cell (WBC) Count: 14,500 cells/mm³ (elevated, indicating leukocytosis)
     - Neutrophils: 82% (left shift, suggesting bacterial infection)
     - Hemoglobin: 13.5 g/dL (normal)
     - Platelets: 230,000 cells/mm³ (normal)
   
   - **C-Reactive Protein (CRP):** 15 mg/L (elevated, marker of inflammation)
   - **Erythrocyte Sedimentation Rate (ESR):** 42 mm/hr (elevated, marker of inflammation)

2. **Microbiology Results:**
   - **Wound Culture and Sensitivity:**
     - Specimen obtained via aspiration of the fluctuant area on the left leg.
     - Growth of *Staphylococcus aureus* identified within 48 hours.
     - Antibiotic sensitivity testing shows susceptibility to methicillin (MSSA) and resistance to penicillin.

   - **Blood Cultures:**
     - Obtained to rule out bacteremia.
     - No growth after 48 hours, indicating no systemic spread of infection.

3. **Imaging Findings:**
   - **Ultrasound of the Left Leg:**
     - Demonstrates a subcutaneous hypoechoic fluid collection measuring approximately 2.5 cm in diameter, consistent with abscess formation.
     - Surrounding cellulitis is evident, with no signs of deeper involvement or osteomyelitis.

4. **Additional Tests:**
   - **Blood Glucose Level:** 160 mg/dL (elevated, but consistent with known diabetes history)
   - **HbA1c:** 7.5% (slightly elevated, indicating suboptimal long-term glucose control)

These diagnostic results confirm the presence of a *Staphylococcus aureus* localized soft tissue infection, manifested as both cellulitis and an abscess, with the bacterium confirmed through wound culture. The absence of bacteremia on blood cultures and the localization on ultrasound suggest that the infection has not become systemic or involved deeper structures. This information will guide the treatment plan, focusing on both antimicrobial therapy and possible drainage of the abscess.

## DIAGNOSIS AND TREATMENT
**Diagnosis:**

- **Primary Diagnosis:** Methicillin-Susceptible *Staphylococcus aureus* (MSSA) infection presenting as cellulitis with associated abscess formation on the left lower leg.

**Treatment Plan:**

1. **Antimicrobial Therapy:**
   - **Empirical Intravenous Antibiotics:** Initiate with Cefazolin 1g IV every 8 hours (selected due to the sensitivity profile showing methicillin susceptibility).
   - **Transition to Oral Therapy:** Once the patient demonstrates clinical improvement, transition to oral Dicloxacillin 500 mg every 6 hours or Cephalexin 500 mg every 6 hours to complete a 7-10 day course.

2. **Surgical Intervention:**
   - **Incision and Drainage (I&D):** Perform bedside or minor surgical procedure to drain the abscess under sterile conditions, which is essential for resolving the localized collection and relieving pressure.

3. **Supportive Care:**
   - Monitor blood glucose levels closely, given the patient's diabetes, and adjust diabetic medications if necessary during the course of treatment to prevent hyperglycemia from complicating the infection.
   - Pain management with acetaminophen as needed, ensuring avoidance of NSAIDs which may impair wound healing or exacerbately elevate blood pressure.

4. **Additional Measures:**
   - Ensure adherence to proper wound care and hygiene with daily dressing changes and elevation of the leg to reduce swelling.
   - Evaluate his glycemic control more broadly and consider referral to an endocrinologist for optimization of diabetes management.

**Prognosis:**

- The prognosis is generally favorable with timely intervention and appropriate antimicrobial therapy. Risk of complications such as further spread or systemic infection (bacteremia) is low with prompt abscess drainage and management.

**Follow-Up Recommendations:**

- **Initial Follow-Up:** Arrange a follow-up visit within 2-3 days to assess treatment response, check wound healing progress, and monitor any potential side effects of antibiotics.
- **Secondary Follow-Up:** A further follow-up in 1-2 weeks post-therapy completion to ensure full resolution of the infection and review diabetes management strategies.
- **Ultrasound Follow-Up (if needed):** Consider repeat imaging only if there is inadequate clinical response or suspicion of deeper or ongoing fluid collection.
- **Preventive Education:** Educate the patient on recognizing early signs of skin infections and maintaining proper foot care due to his diabetic status.

By implementing this comprehensive treatment and follow-up plan, James Whitfield should experience a full recovery while minimizing the risk of recurrence or complications associated with his underlying conditions.
